Why This Track Exists

Poetry teaches you precision. Mathematics teaches you wonder. And the gothic — your literature — teaches the third thing: that knowledge has a price, and the people who pay it are the interesting ones. Victor Frankenstein is a cautionary tale about research ethics. The Underground Man picks a fight with arithmetic. Ivan Ilyich dies arguing with a syllogism.

Every program you're aiming at reads your prose before it reads your proofs. So this track is built like the others: named texts, exact weeks, artifacts due — a seminar you teach yourself, in the canon you actually love.

Continuous Practices · Every Week, All Semester

The Commonplace Book

Daily transcription of passages worth keeping — by hand. The oldest scholarly technology there is.

Memorization

One piece by heart every two weeks — 12 by December: Ozymandias, Dickinson, Dante's opening terzina, a soliloquy…

The Weekly Artifact

One finished piece per week, alternating poem / essay. Finished means revised at least twice.

Sunday Colloquium

Thirty minutes, aloud: defend the week's reading, recite, read your own work as if to a room.

The Six Units · July — December
IJuly · wks 1–5

The Romantic Flame

The sublime, the lyric, and the six great Romantics — plus Dickinson, their strangest heir.
▾
Primary Texts
  • "The Tyger", "The Lamb", "London", "Auguries of Innocence" — William Blake
  • "Tintern Abbey", "Intimations of Immortality", Preface to Lyrical Ballads — William Wordsworth
  • "The Rime of the Ancient Mariner", "Kubla Khan", "Christabel" I — S. T. Coleridge
  • The odes + letters — John Keats (incl. the negative capability letter)
  • "Ozymandias", "Ode to the West Wind", "Mont Blanc" — Percy Bysshe Shelley
  • "Darkness", "She Walks in Beauty", Manfred I — Lord Byron
  • "After great pain…", "I heard a Fly buzz…", "Because I could not stop for Death", "One need not be a Chamber…", "I felt a Funeral, in my Brain" — Emily Dickinson
Theory & Craft
  • Enquiry into the Sublime and Beautiful (selections) — Edmund Burke
  • A Poetry Handbook — Mary Oliver (complete, week 1)
  • Poetic Meter and Poetic Form — Paul Fussell (meter chapters)
Skills
  • Scansion, iambic pentameter, ballad stanza, common meter
  • Sonnet anatomy and the volta
  • The sublime vs. the beautiful; the Byronic hero
The Weeks
WK 1
Oliver (complete) · Blake, the four poems · Wordsworth, Preface
Due: scansion exercise + first poem
WK 2
Wordsworth, "Tintern Abbey" + "Intimations" · Burke on the sublime
Due: a landscape poem where the landscape looks back
WK 3
Coleridge — the Mariner entire, aloud; "Kubla Khan"; "Christabel" I
Due: ballad-stanza poem + 300w on the Mariner's frame
WK 4
Keats, four odes + letters · Shelley, the three poems
Due: an ode; memorize "Ozymandias"
WK 5
Byron, "Darkness" + Manfred I · Dickinson, the five poems
Due: ars poetica + two pieces recited by heart

Keats wrote the odes at twenty-three. You are allowed to take them personally.

IIAugust · wks 6–10 · ★

The Gothic Imagination

The heart of the track — your literature. Frankenstein, Poe, and the Brontës' haunted moors.
▾
Primary Texts
  • Frankenstein (1818 text, complete) + the 1831 introduction — Mary Shelley
  • "The Fall of the House of Usher", "The Tell-Tale Heart", "The Masque of the Red Death", "Ligeia" · "The Raven", "Annabel Lee" — Edgar Allan Poe

  • Wuthering Heights (complete) — Emily Brontë
The Long Read: Jane Eyre (Charlotte Brontë) accompanies weeks 10–14 at ~fifty pages a week; its seminar closes Unit III, week 14.
Skills & Questions
  • Frame narration: Walton's letters; Lockwood & Nelly Dean
  • The double, the uncanny, the found manuscript
  • The sublime landscape as character — Alps, moor, maelström
  • The creature's eloquence: why the monster speaks in Milton
  • Unity of effect (Poe) vs. organic form (Coleridge)
The Weeks
WK 6
Frankenstein, vol. I + the 1831 introduction ("my hideous progeny")
Due: one page in Victor's journal-voice
WK 7
Frankenstein, vols. II–III — the creature speaks
Due: essay — the first myth of the scientist, 1,500w
WK 8
Poe — four tales, two poems, and "The Philosophy of Composition"
Due: close reading #1 — "The Raven" by Poe's own method: a poem built like a proof
WK 9
Wuthering Heights, first half — the double narration begins
Due: a poem in the gothic mode
WK 10
Wuthering Heights, finish · begin Jane Eyre (long read)
Due: close reading #2 — Lockwood & Nelly, the untrustworthy frame

Mary Shelley was nineteen when she dreamed the creature. The gothic is young people writing about the cost of knowledge — it was always your genre.

IIISeptember · wks 11–14

The Epic Descent

Homer and Dante — the underworld visited twice, twenty centuries apart.
▾
Primary Texts
  • The Odyssey (Emily Wilson trans.) — books I–XII, XXI–XXIV, with Book XI read twice — Homer
  • Inferno (complete; Hollander or Musa trans.) — Dante Alighieri
Method
  • "Odysseus' Scar" (Mimesis, ch. 1) — Erich Auerbach
Skills & Questions
  • Epic simile, epithet, invocation; oral formula
  • Terza rima and the architecture of the Inferno
  • Contrapasso: punishment as theorem
  • Homeric brightness vs. biblical depth (Auerbach)
The Weeks
WK 11
Odyssey I–VIII · Auerbach, "Odysseus' Scar"
Due: an invocation to a muse of your own
WK 12
Odyssey IX–XII (the descent) + XXI–XXIV (the homecoming)
Due: essay — Odyssey XI as the first ghost story, 1,200w
WK 13
Inferno I–XVII — the gate, Francesca, the burning city
Due: a canto of your own in terza rima (~40 lines)
WK 14
Inferno XVIII–XXXIV · Jane Eyre seminar closes
Due: essay — Dante's architecture of justice; memorize the opening terzina

Odysseus goes down to the dead to ask directions. Dante goes down to map the whole thing. That escalation is Western literature in one move.

The Weeks

WK 16

Dostoevsky spent four years in Siberia and came back arguing with utilitarian arithmetic. He is the patron saint of everyone who knows a valid argument can still be wrong.

Where This Fits

Poe claims he wrote "The Raven" like a mathematical proof — test that claim in week 8 while you're writing real ones. The Underground Man attacks 2×2=4 the same autumn you're proving what arithmetic can and cannot do; Sisyphus meets Gödel in week 22; and Frankenstein is the oldest essay ever written on the ethics of the lab. The gothic is not an escape from the science — it is the science, haunted — and it's why your statement of purpose will not read like anyone else's.
